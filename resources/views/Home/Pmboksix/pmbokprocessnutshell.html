These ideas (expanded in the hyperlinks and the steps below) involve some degree of lateral thinking, depending on the depth of your understanding of project management and its processes.

Often, when running projects in the real world the projects (per the Chaos Reports) are simply BADLY RUN PROJECTS.  I.e. Project Process is not followed!
There is also often little put into place in terms of the initiating, planning or monitoring and controls processes and procedures recommended in the PMBOK or other project methods (PRINCE2 Agile, SCRUM, DSDM etc.). 

These projects are also allowed to be run at low levels of Capability and Maturity, often firmly at CML1, by an ingorant executive (of project process) who's duty it must be to always ensure that projects are well managed and governance is firmly in place (for the budget / money allocated).

In this regard you are invited to think again about RISK and WASTE or scarce resources and specifically about the wisdom from Aesop's fable about the Tortoise and the Hare.  I.e. political pressure to JUST DO IT appears to disregard the step by step nature of project process which should always be managed.  This, in a nut shell, is what differentiates professional project management at Capability Maturity Level 2 from JUST DO IT at Capability Maturity Level 1.

A concept that can assist you to appreciate the above is DDUF, EDUG and NDUF.  I.e. in traditional project management detailed documentation (plan etc.) is required up front (DDUF), in the Dynamic Systems Development Method (Agile PM) - the oldest form of agile, enough documentation is required (EDUF), while looser forms of agile require no documentation up front (EDUF).  In the PMBOK Dashboard Planning and Monotoring and Control sit on either side of the EXECUTION process group.  Dr W. Edwards Deming, the process guru behind the Capability Maturity model has a cycle called the Deming Cycle.  I.e. Plan, Do, Check, Act.  The Capability Maturity model (from Carnegie Mellon University in the USA) understands that without Project Planning (PP process) and Project Monitoring and Control (PMC) a project is unable to achieve CM Level 2 and is at CM Level 1.  There is higher Risk and Waste and lower Productivity and Quality at Level 1 than Level 2.  I.e. how do you want to be remembered?

Step 1: How to use the PMBOK Dashboard - a short overview

The Project Management Body of Knowledge Dashboard allows you to easily (and systematically) access the essence of the 756 page PMBOK Guide.
This BOK (Body of Knowledge) is comprised of 49 project processes (you can think of processes as building blocks / or interconnected pipes facilitating correct flow of project process).
These 49 project processes also form connected nodes (on the dashboard) that link up Process Groups (across the top of the dashboard) and Knowledge Areas (that run down the left hand side of the dashboard). Can you see these nodes and how they tie together the Process Groups and Knowledge Areas of the PMBOK?

Underneath each process node (4.1, 4.2, 4.3 etc.) are the process: => [INPUTS] =>, => [TOOLS & TECHNIQUES] =>, and => [OUTPUTS] =>. 
(Clicking here for PMWay's home page so you can click a NODE and see its underlying / supporting ITTO's.)

The PMBOK Dashboard allows you to break down the complexity of the PMBOK from overall project summary (the Dashboard) to detail within a process step (within the 49 process) and then (using PMWay website) you can drill down into each process Inputs, Tools and Techniques and Outputs (ITTO's).  Ultimately, at any point in time while running your project, you should be able to put a pin into the dashboard showing EXACTLY where your project is AS AT NOW!
Ultimately (as a PMWay client) you are able to drill down further from each ITTO into the detail residing within the PMBOK Guide.

Tailoring notes (the ability to make project management sweet, simple, lean and agile) is an essential skill for the Project Management Professional which can and should be undertaken for each process.
I.e. this is where you keep a record of what you are using of the process (its particular ITTO's), in what detail these are to be used, and what you have decided can be left out. 
Remember while BEST PRACTICE is best, BEST PRACTICAL is better!

Due to the fact that the PMBOK is proprietary for members of the Project Management Institute, access to the PMBOK (the book itself) can only be granted within PMWay (under my auspices) of which a professional and student relationship is available.
However, even without my assistance, a wealth of knowledge (I suggest most of the Knowledge you require for the PMP exam or what is needed to run successful projects) is readily available to the casual visitor within this web without you having to purchase anything from PMWay.

Step 2:  The PMBOK Project Management Processes - in more detail

Take a look at the PMBOK Dashboard teaching overlay above. 
Now, each time you run a project simply, but systematically, work from the TOP LEFT of the dashboard (down first) and then across (handling each of the processes systematically in turn) move systematically across the dashboard until you end up at the TOP RIGHT of the dashboard at Close Project or Phase! 
It is as simple as that!

You can, if you like, think about pouring water into the top left of the dashboard, into process 4.1, and having this water flow down to process 13.1 and then, up to and across to process 4.2 and down and back up again and etc.
I.e. both starting processes are in the INITIATING PROCESS GROUP) and then moving this water (flowing across and down the next process group) through all the processes until you have a workable PLAN and DEVELOPED PRELIMINARY SCHEDULE (before moving across to EXECUTION - where you will baseline this as a FINAL SCHEDULE) and Monitoring and Controlling Delivery of Planned Work / Managing Change Control, Budget, Communications, Budget and etc., finally getting to (and out of) process 4.7 (Close Project or Phase).

But remember that the PMBOK is not a waterfall!

You can move through the processes both left to right and back to left again.  Also up and down / from beginning to end of the dashboard. 
I.e. You can turn around and go backwards (against the process flow) when needed when, for example, CHANGE CONTROL (4.6) or other process changes are needed. 
In addition (as you can see with process 4.7- Close Project or PHASE) shorter project sprints are available off the PMBOK dashboard.  In this way you can iterate and increment, step by step, towards a final solution.
This simple fact is often ignored by people who criticize the PMI, saying it is a waterfall methodology.

Always remember that while the PMBOK can be used as a method, it is in fact a Body of Knowledge on the best way to run projects.
In this regard you can think firstly of the PMBOK as a list of ingredients that can be used to run projects and then, using the process groups, a method (recipe) that tells the cook where and when each ingredient should be used. 
In addition, know that PMBOK version 6 is now fully Agile and Lean compatible with discussion in each process about how the process can be used within Agile, Iterative and Adaptive environments!

Now let us, by way of example, move from Left to Right across (and through) the PMBOK dashboard (project processes) in a little more detail:

Essentially, to run successful projects (after you have a Project Management Appointment Form signed and a sponsor appointed [and the sponsor should sign the appointment of you as a PM form!), you must start with either process 4.1 (The Project Charter) or process 13.1 (The Stakeholder Register) which activities fall firmly within the Initiation Process Group.  These two processes must be completed before moving into the Planning Processes.  Completion may only need to be at Version 1.  I.e. you can always come back and add to and improve your list of Stakeholders and even update the Charter if required. Remember the concept of iteration and incrimination.  I.e. using version control you can put 4 stakeholders into your stakeholder register in version 1.  Then after a few days you hear about another stakeholder.  You add them and update the contact details for one of the fist 4 as version 2.  I.e. in this way your stakeholder register is growing and adjusting as you pin down you stakeholders. As long as your sponsor is ok with and approves this.  Also approval, while a signature is often required, the simple fact of a typed project log (short record) of what you have done, emailed to the sponsor (stakeholders) and then saved will suffice as evidence that you have 'improved the project documents / project knowledge' on your project.  I.e. making your running of the project more agile and lean. 

However, and again, do not proceed to the Planning Process Group (from the Initiating Process Group) until these two documents are completed and approved - no matter how much they bully you to do this! 

 Remember that in order to get the Charter Signed one of these stakeholders must be accountable and be in the position to kick start the project from a governance perspective.  I.e. think bus or train ticket collector.  If your charter (ticket) is not punched you cannot ride on this project bus or train!  So do not start the journey until your ticket is approved and punched as valid by your sponsor.

To explain this very important PROJECT INITIATION concept further:  In the PRINCE2 Agile (PRojects IN Controlled Environments) methodology, a Project Board is responsible for the project and they appoint and manage the Project Manager, who's job it is to serve them.  I.e. they must approve the project from a Business Case perspective (that it will add value to the bottom line) before it is allowed to run.  They must also approve the project products that the project will be producing.  At the end of the project the Board must check and assess that value has in fact been delivered through the project via its project products!

Tailoring and its importance!

Remember that 'tailoring' (per the PMBOK guide) means that you must look at each of the 49 processes and decide what you need to do from the perspective of process ITTO's (using your knowledge from the PMBOK guide) that you select to run your particular project.  Know that from a LEAN and AGILE perspective the following rule must always apply:  While BEST PRACTICE is understood, BEST PRACTICAL is better!  
Therefore the SKILL of the Project Management Professional is in the ability to tailor their project towards LEAN and AGILITY!  That said, NEVER trade away your ability to land a project safely while responding to pressure you will surely face to make your project go faster!  The fable of the tortoise and the hare from Aesop and going off 'half-cocked' is 100% applicable to this way of thinking.  Some ideas from PMWay on this are here.

The clickable dashboard (Level 2 under the dashboard) has a tailoring section for each process where you can add your tailoring ideas (decisions).  You can also print these out to pdf if you have adobe writer loaded on your machine.  I.e. as an audit trail to be used in audit or for later reference.

Therefore, first Initiate the project correctly by completing the Development of the Project Charter (4.1) and creating a list of Identified Stakeholders (13.1).

Then (and this is really important) PLAN the work before you start doing (EXECUTING) the work!

Here is something you may not have thought of.  Do you know about the Deming Cycle.  This is PLAN, DO, CHECK and then ACT. 
Now, if you take a look at the PMBOK dashboard, you will see that it uses exactly this approach!  I.e. PLAN EXECUTE MONITOR & CONTROL and CHANGE CONTROL!
I.e. a dream without a plan is just a wish.

NB: This plan does not need to be WAR & PEACE!  In fact, if your Stakeholders agree, it can be a single page long!

I do suggest that your plan follow the Knowledge Areas (along the left side of the PMBOK Dashboard) so that you make sure you cover how you will handle the processes within each of these.  If you have signed up with PMWay I have a template that you can work that is structured according to this method.

Now lets look at how this (PLANNING PROCESS GROUP) works in more detail:

Can you see that the Project Management Plan 4.2 is the sum total of all of the processes (and Knowledge Area Plans) underneath it!
I.e. 4.2 = 5.1, 6.1, 7.1, 8.1, 9.1, 10.1, 11.1, 12.1 & 13.2]).  Obviously the SCOPE PLAN (5.1) is the summary of the processes under 5.1 which are 5.2 =  Collect Requirements, 5.3 = Define Requirements and 5.4 =  Create WBS (Work Breakdown Structure). 
If you think in terms of sub plans to create the total plan then 5 = SCOPE, 6 = SCHEDULE, 7 = COST, 8 = QUALITY, 9 = RESOURCES, 10 = COMMUNICATIONS, 11= RISK, 12 =  PROCUREMENT AND 13 = STAKEHOLDERS which all build 4.1 the Project Management Plan.

Only when the planning is done will you start to DO THE WORK as you DIRECT and MANAGE (4.3) project work (EXECUTION) and MONITOR & CONTROL (4.4) the project to ensure that you remain on track with your PLAN.  Project Knowledge (a resource to pin down knowledge learned while running your project / which can be used to better run and assist with future projects etc.) is handled in process 4.4.

This 'managing the flow' of processes systematically is very important to ensure you are able to complete the project as you move to ('successfully jump the planned project delivery gap'). 
A key concept to understand is that of baseline.  I.e. before you start a project your system or project environment (configuration) will be at a present baseline.  The project plan aims, via an approved Business Case, to move this baseline to a new and improved baseline.  In a system build for example, if the new baseline proves to be a problem then it should be possible to move back to a previous baseline.  This concept, and the Capability Maturity Level 2, 3 and 4 concept of bi directional traceability is illustrated in PMWay here.

Baseline is also used in terms of the project schedule (Process 6.5 - Developed Schedule).  I.e. this plan (schedule) pulls together the who, what, when that is needed to get to the new baseline.  Typically project management software can lock down the plan into a baseline so that variances against the plan can be used when managing the project.  Earned Value Management is all about bringing the project in on time, within budget and with an agreed scope delivered.  While agile will try to circumvent the need to do EVM and monitor and control the plan (Iron Triangle), PMWay suggests that the minute a project starts to run costs are being expended and delivery must be planned and delivered, or else...

PMWay suggests that an inability of accounting (and the business driven annual accounting function / budgeting cycle) to adjust to a budget already approved for a project is the root cause for blame for the negative perception of project management and why projects are often run at Capability Maturity level 1.  I.e. low maturity appears to exist in this area which insists on everyone JUST Doing IT at all costs (CM L1) to bring in a project, even though SCOPE etc., has changed.  Why can a project manager not go back and ask for a review of their project budget without fear of an hysterical executive?  I.e. STOP the bus if the SCOPE changes and adjust the project plan accordingly, which must include an adjustment of the COST KNOWLEDGE AREA (and other associated Knowledge Areas) of the PMBOK Dashboard leading to a new plan and new schedule.  I.e. this schedule is not the old schedule and cannot be expected to exist on the old (approved at that time) budget.  I.e. else, if the new scope / budget is not acceptible to the executive, then the only other option is to simply terminate the project!

By means of example: You and your family decide to drive to a holiday destination. Then, mid holiday, which is not as great as you expected, you decide to change your plan and spend time with relatives 4 hours drive further along the coast than your holiday destination. I.e. your plan changes and as driver (head of the family etc.), you, after buy in from your family (spouse, kids etc.), simply spend the extra money for the change in experience, if you can afford it.

If change in the project plan is required then the Change Control Board (CCB) and your Change Control Process (4.6) is used to ensure the sub plans (which add up to the total Project Management Plan 4.2) are updated to include new deliverables (work packages) in the revised Work Breakdown Structure and Schedule etc., are all in sync with the new (agreed / approved) requirements.

Following the processes the Project Manager will be able to reach the end of the project (per latest agreed Project Management Plan and the project can be CLOSED (4.7).
